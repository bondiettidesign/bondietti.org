The Snek

April 20241,201 words

The snake was so proud of itself. It just shed its old skin, and the feeling of the crisp forest air on new skin was a pleasure it never tired of. The cool morning breeze was a bit too chilly though, and after all that hard work, the snake was famished. It set out for the day looking for a warm spot and something to eat. Before long, the snake crept up a creek, where its neighbor, a young otter, was playing at sliding down the steep, slick creek banks. As the otter got to the top of the bank, he had a big stretch and shook all the dewy drops of spring water off of his shiny fur. The chilly water droplets felt like eagle talons on the snake’s sensitive new skin.

“Hey, stupid otter!” said the angry snake. “How dare you shake your water off on me! Don’t you know how it feels to have icy cold water on fresh new skin?”

“Awww buddy, I’m sorry,” said the young otter. He gave the snake’s head a little pat with his fore-paw. “What you really need to do is just jump right into that water and shock that bad feeling right out of your system. Come on, let’s go!” The otter gave his neighbor a big otter-hug, then he flopped on his back for a ride down the muddy embankment.

“No!” said the snake. It bit the otter firmly on the nose.

The otter let go of the snake and ran off to his den where he howled in pain all morning and then died. The snake slithered into the cool grass once again to rub the sandy creek grit off of its soft, shiny snake skin.

“Serves him right,” said the snake. “As soon as I find a nice rock to sun myself on, it’s the soft life for me. I can’t wait to lay there all day admiring my new skin.” The snake slithered down the trail until they found a perfect, sunny rock. Well… almost perfect. The rock had a perturbing presence in the form of the snake’s neighbor, an old bear, napping in the afternoon sun.

“Hey, old bear!” said the snake. “That’s my rock!”

The bear didn’t do much more than twitch a fly off of his ear.

“I’ve just changed my skin, and I need that rock. Now, move!”

The serpent rattled off a warning with its tail.

“But snake, my friend, my old bones are tired.” The bear huffed an exhausted sigh. “Besides, why should I listen to you when I was here first?”

“Stupid bear,” said the snake, “Don’t you know snakes need to sun themselves on warm rocks after they change their skin?”

“No. I suppose I didn’t know that,” said the bear.

“Why don’t you care about snakes?” hissed the snake.

The snake bit the bear on the nose. The bear sprang up and ran home to hide in his cave. He cried all the rest of the day while the snake’s venom slowly did its work. The serpent then spent the entire afternoon content in the sun upon a warm rock.

“Serves him right,” said the snake.

The snake set out the next morning slightly thicker of skin, and absolutely famished. It snuck and slithered across the forest floor looking for a victim until it came upon a cougar. The old cougar was playing with her food, a perfectly plump mouse, near the base of a large tree. The snake had hunted all day and could wait no longer.

“Hey, old cougar,” said the snake, “I’m hungry. Give me that mouse.”

“I hunted all day for this mouse, my friend,” said the cougar. She felt sorry for the hungry little snake, “How about we share it?” The old cougar chomped the mouse in half and padded nearer to the snake as she delivered the meal by its tail.

“Stupid cougar!” said the snake. “You should already know we snakes eat our mice whole!” The serpent bit the cougar on the nose, and she cried out and dropped the mouse. She climbed the large tree and rested on its largest branch, crying all morning while the venom did the work. She died in the afternoon while both halves of the mouse worked their way down the contented, sleeping snake’s gullet.

The snake awoke early that evening to a thump, they looked over to see a slimy pellet full of bones and fur. The snake didn’t move a muscle as they scanned the canopy above. There, on a branch above them was an old, gray owl.

“Whooooooo, whooooo, whoooo did this! Whoooo killed my friend?” The owl, full of grief, searched her friend for clues until she found two tiny little fang marks on the cougar’s nose. “Only a snake would do this!” The owl flew off to investigate the rest of the scene.

The snake slunk off to the sounds of the owl’s cries and hurried towards its favorite outcropping of rocks to hide. On its way, a different shadow floated across the ground. The shadow landed on the very top of the outcropping of rocks the snake was headed for. It was an eagle who had smelled the carrion in the neighborhood and swooped by to investigate. The snake changed directions and slithered down towards the creek, looking for safe shelter until eventually, it grew tired in the reeds, and so began to cry itself to sleep.

“Who is that crying out there?” a friendly voice called out from near the creek bed.

“It is me! Snake!” said the snake. “Who are you?”

“I am also snek!” said the voice. “Why are you crying?”

“I’m trying to escape from an owl and an eagle,” The scared snake poked its head out from the reeds. “I have no place to hide and I am scared they will eat me.”

“My poor friend,” said the voice. “No snek should go without a place to hide. You can hide with me!”

“Really?” said the snake. “I would be so grateful! I’ve just changed my skin, and these stupid forest animals just don’t understand what it’s like to be a snake!”

“I understand,” said the voice. “We sneks have a rough life. I know a hiding place near the waterfall. It is in a beaver’s house. She doesn’t need it anymore… because I bit her. Haha!”

“Haha!” said the snake. “Okay! I will live in a beaver’s house with you. Take me!

“I’m afraid you are going to have to swim, my snek friend. Follow my voice!”

“Oh, heck,” said the snake. “Maybe cold is better than dead.”

“That’s right!” said the voice, “Live sneks are best.”

The snake almost began to feel a shred of remorse, and wondered if the advice from the otter was right. Perhaps they should have jumped into the water this morning. Now it would have to be cold anyways, and in the dark!

The snake visualized the warm beaver den and the cozy naps it would take with its new snake friend. It closed its eyes, took a deep snake breath, and plunged straight into the gaping mouth of a wily catfish.

The end
