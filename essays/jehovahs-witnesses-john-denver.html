Jehovah’s Witnesses and the Legend of John Denver

A story I was told as a child, and what it took to unlearn it

October 2023

I can conjure up an image of a house I lived in as a child for almost a year. It had shabby yellow wallpaper with brown trim, and I remember a gigantic, inconveniently placed air-vent for the furnace that would burn your bare feet when you walked across it. We had a sea-green colored couch with embossed fabric, and I have a memory of running my fingers along the almost three-dimensional leaves and vines. Some of my first memories in that house are moments when my mom and I would cuddle together on the couch and listen to music on a vinyl record player. Of all the songs and records she picked out, the singer who stood out to me most was John Denver. One of his songs I remember enjoying was the song of the lands we lived in, Rocky Mountain High, which taught me a sense of place. There was also mom’s favorite, Annie’s Song, such a unique expression of love. Denver is unique amongst songwriters in the way he can express emotions like joy, happiness, pride and love. The idealism in the way he writes his songs is very much needed for us humans, who can tend to grow cynical. John Denver is also the main character of a myth told amongst my peers during my time spent as a Jehovah’s Witness.

I need to explain this religion a bit, to get you to understand the setting. They are a revisionist branch of Christianity who believe their worship practices closely match those of the first century Christian congregations. Jehovah’s Witnesses do not believe in the trinity doctrine and consider God and Jesus to be separate and distinct individuals. They believe in door-to-door preaching, although more recently they are relaxing their expectations of their followers to do so, preferring carts on street corners. They do not celebrate holidays, which they believe have Pagan origins, including birthdays. They do not use images in their worship of God. They don’t believe the soul survives death, and do not believe in the doctrine of hellfire.

Jehovah’s Witnesses interpret the Bible event of Armageddon literally—sulfurous fireballs, hailstones and all—and they believe this apocalypse is imminent, although they have constantly moved the ‘imminent’ goalposts for over a century. When my family started going to their church ‘meetings’ in the late eighties, I was told I would not graduate from high school before Armageddon happened. We were told to be ready to be persecuted by the outside world for our faith, and if we were obedient to our faith, we would be rewarded by surviving Armageddon to live forever in a paradise on earth (Psalms 37:1-11). Although it was not something we would talk about at your door on Saturday morning, most of us speculated that after the end of the world, we would be digging pits to chuck all the unbelievers’ bones into after the carrion birds picked you clean (Revelations 19:17), and then we would eventually go on to build fancy dream houses of our own design, where we could pet lions and snakes (Isaiah 11:8), whom Jehovah would tame for us.

Before we see how John Denver’s utopian lyrics work into all of this, we need to learn more about the kinds of folks I was spending time with during those early years. We were as poor as anyone could be. We had just escaped from my stepfather and moved to a small farm town from an old ranch. For years after, we would drive twenty-four miles to the next town with an elderly couple, Mae and Clarence, to Jehovah’s Witness meetings, three times a week. That’s an hour of driving time, two hours of meeting time, three times a week, plus getting cleaned up and dressed in our church outfits. We all packed ourselves into a four-door sedan where a single mother, her sullen oldest child, two cranky babies, and two folks who survived the Great Depression all shared the same air. We took this trip rain, shine, or white-out and black-ice blizzard, waiting for God to kill all the bad people (you), so we could be happy.

Mae was the kind of lady to flirt with me during the car trips to meetings while we were all squished together. She sometimes joked about how she hoped she died before Armageddon happened so she could marry me. See… by Mae’s logic, ‘Till Death Do Us Part’ was an actual contract, and if she died before Armageddon happened, she could be free to marry whomever she chose after God resurrected her on a paradise earth. All she had to do was convince me to stay single that long. I couldn’t see it happening from where I was sitting, but she seemed serious about the one-and-done with Clarence.

Among the conversations going on in that car ride, there were speculations about how the great flood of Noah’s day must have happened, and several ways we could reconcile how Methuselah must have lived so long to what we were learning about modern science. Along with our speculations and reconciliations, various myths were passed along in car rides, such as the rule that Jehovah’s Witness kids shouldn’t own Smurfs toys because they supposedly attracted demons. In fact, there was a tale about a child, who supposedly lived just a few congregations over, who had been allowed to keep a Smurf doll. The damned thing came to life in the middle of a meeting, realized it was in a house of God, hopped off the chair and ran out the Kingdom Hall doors!

Mae also told me the most heart-wrenching story ever. John Denver did not like Jehovah’s Witnesses. According to the story, Denver asked all the Jehovah’s Witnesses presently attending one of his concerts to stand up, which they did. Mister Denver then told all my new spiritual brothers and sisters how he disliked their religion and its policies and asked them to leave!

Later, during an appearance on The Tonight Show, Johnny Carson asked the singer if he had, in fact, kicked some ‘witnesses’ out of his concert, and Denver affirmed that he did. Mr. Denver explained that he didn’t appreciate how Jehovah’s Witnesses didn’t salute the flag, go to war, or take blood transfusions, and he was in protest of their belief system. Mr. Carson said he employed some witnesses, and they were very fine people whose literature he enjoyed reading. Carson then asked the singer to leave the stage at that very moment as punishment for his callous behavior towards God’s chosen people. Mae was very happy to report that Mr. Denver got his just deserts.

This news crushed my idealistic child soul. Gone were all those warm memories of listening to my favorite music on the couch as a young child being cuddled by his mother. Gone was the joy of listening to Rocky Mountain High when taking day trips to Glacier National Park. Now those sweet songs soured in my gut. Every time a John Denver song played on the radio was a reminder of how dark the world is. In my child’s mind, this singer attacked a religion that just saved me from my alcoholic stepdad. If this man is an enemy of God, just think of the horrible kinds of people whose true selves are hidden by such sweet words. What of Mr. Rogers? Bob Ross? Could their kind smiles harbor demons?

That life-altering moment had been sublimated throughout the years into general darkness whenever I heard Denver’s music. Eventually after leaving the Jehovah’s Witnesses, I forgot the source of the feeling. Even a decade after quitting the religion, packing my life up, and moving away from Montana and out to the Pacific Northwest, I could not get past that sour feeling every time I heard him on a playlist.

These dark feelings over John Denver’s music have only unpacked themselves during some more recent moments of solitude where I was trying to learn how to write short fiction stories. I scoured the internet to see if the John Denver tale was told elsewhere. I hope you will be glad to know that the incident between John Denver and the witnesses never happened, nor did he ever get kicked off The Tonight Show. It was a hoax, and it turns out he’s not even the only entertainer accused of this, Don Ho and Neil Diamond also being implicated. I found similar tales have been told inside other religions.

I hope poor old Mae won’t be too disappointed to wake up in the paradisaic resurrection someday to find that eleven-year-old me isn’t waiting to offer her his hand in marriage. She might also be disappointed that I have done another thing discouraged by the Jehovah’s Witnesses, which is attend college. Witnesses are taught that they should be no part of the outside world, and that associating with ‘worldly’ people is a danger to their faith. Post-secondary education is considered spiritually dangerous and strongly discouraged. You might learn such things as evolution, biology, philosophy and psychology. You might even write about your experiences of said religion. What if you didn’t have good things to say?

It has been a decade and a half since I left that world behind. I try not to be too resentful of my old religion these days. There are so many genuinely good people following that religion, just trying to get by. I miss so many individuals I used to spend time with, and I miss having a third space that does not cost money. I am grateful they don’t participate in politics. I am still idealistically aligned with their pacifism. I am glad their little children do not have to knock on other people’s doors, handing out church pamphlets anymore, and I want all my lost birthday and Christmas presents back. As far as happy endings to strange stories like this one go, all I have is this: here before you is a college essay, written by a man of a relatively free mind, who still loves his mom and the music of John Denver.
