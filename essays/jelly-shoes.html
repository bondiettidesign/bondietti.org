Jelly Shoes

I can still remember the smell of a certain kind of shoe from my childhood. The little girls in my grade would wear them on the playground. The shoe was a translucent, colorful shoe made completely out of a gel like plastic material. I remember seeing the shoes at the department store one day, so I picked them up to take a look. They were squishy and sticky. They were decorated with three-dimensional shapes like butterflies, flowers and paisleys. Off-gassing chemical compounds from the manufacturing process, they smelled like a combination of formaldehyde and rubbing alcohol, and my sinuses stung and tingled like I had pool water in my nose. Who would even want to wear these sticky smelly shoes? Shoes that collected dirt from the playground and lint from the laundry room. Shoes offered in various shades of puke green, salmon-egg pink, barely blue and toxic orange, which stained the socks of the fashion victim wearing them through the decorative holes designed to vent the wearer’s sweaty little feet.

It was always the evil ones who wore jelly shoes. Evil girls like Annie Bobannie, who was a dead ringer for the Annie of Broadway fame, with a meaner looking scowl. She was the first girl I met at Headstart. I met her when she blindsided me with a shove into the dirt, bending over me to say something about ‘snips and snails and puppy dog tails’ before running away while I plucked out the bits and pieces of gravel that were imbedded in the palms of my hands.

Another jelly shoe villain was Brandy Bobandy. Her name was right next to mine on all the class lists. She was always putting her coat on my coat rack and her backpack in my locker for the first few days of school each year. Gold star stickers were awarded for hanging your coat on the correct rack in Headstart, and jelly shoe wearing Brandy cost me a few.

The worst jelly shoe villain of all was Rikki Bobikki. She had hair so blonde it was almost translucent. She had ghostly blue eyes, and skin so tanned you’d wonder if she ever spent time indoors. I remember getting lost playing in her basement which was jam packed with cardboard boxes and papers. She was constantly tunnelling down to the bottom of various piles of things so she could show me her toys, but once found, she was always on to the next toy without even slowing down.

Rikki finally drew my contempt when she and her jelly shoe evil twin, motormouth Mary, shoved me on the playground in second grade. I chased Rikki down to shove her back and she started screaming bloody murder over it. The girls tag-teamed me verbally with ‘Boys don’t hit girls!’ and “Brian’s a bully!” I was certain my school days were over, and that I would spend the rest of my life in the principal’s office writing don’t shove girls on the chalkboard. Thankfully, no teachers came to their rescue, but I learned my lesson:

Jelly shoe girls are the enemy.
