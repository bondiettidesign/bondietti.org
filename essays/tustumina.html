Tustumina

January 2024

I had already followed my fathers requirements on attire: neoprene thermal underwear, thick wool socks, handmade leather mountaineer’s pac boots with a felt liner and indestructible rubber sole, plain cotton tee shirt. a thick flannel button up shirt. A zip-up hoodie, an ultra thick Carhartt parka from the days before it was a fashion brand, a plain winter cap, and poly gloves underneath leather ranchers gloves.

There were also his instructions on trusting the horses to do their job: including allowing the horses to find a base camp they visited once or twice a year on their own instincts. He trusted his horses enough to fall asleep while in the saddle. The problem was that he was training his newest and youngest horse to lead the rest of the pack string. It took a wrong turn while we napped in our saddles and we ended up on top of an exposed mountain ridge surrounded by wind-blown tundra and the sun had just gone down.

For the audacity of being there, when we could just as have well been sipping hot cocoa next to a fireplace in a warm log cabin, Karma gifted us an early winter storm. The snow set in so fast that within minutes it felt like we were on a different planet. We couldn’t see anything but white. We couldn’t move another step. My dad feared taking a wrong turn and the whole pack string tumbling off the side of a mountain. He made the choice to set up camp immediately and figure out where we were when the sun came up the next morning.

He instructed me on exactly how to set up camp and unsaddle the horses for the evening without causing hypothermia. The basic rules were to take care of the horses first and yourselves second. For the next hour or two, we slowly undid ten horses’ worth of materials in a blizzard in the dark. It would be pure cruelty to expect the horses to carry hundreds of pounds each up the side of a mountain and then stand in the weather for an entire evening bearing that load. The tedious way we unpacked them was important because the horses were all sweaty underneath their pack saddles. We could only loosen the saddles a bit and remove the camp supplies then let them sit for fifteen minutes or so to acclimate to the weather. We repeated these steps for the saddles, and again for the saddle blankets. Next my dad hitched all his horses together and covered them up with large canvas camp tarps. Only after all of this did we feed and water the horses and finally ourselves before we set up our own camp, which for the evening, would consist of a basic hikers tent sized to fit two people. We heated ourselves for a very short time by a compact gas camp stove, just long enough to boil water to pour in our Nalgene bottles. We used these as a heat source in our sleeping bags for the evening. We couldn’t leave the stove on for much longer than for fear of carbon monoxide poisoning. We climbed into our sleeping bags with all our layers still on. I could feel the cold through every barrier between the air and my toes. It was so cold that when my father woke once in the night to light the stove and reheat the air in the tent, seeing the light from the flame flickering on the stove was enough for me to imagine my toes warming up.

We woke the next morning with our digits intact, all the horses were fine, and we performed the night routine in reverse. Loading the horses with saddles and supplies, and finally ourselves and headed back down the mountain into the Tustumina region of the Kenai National Wildlife Refuge.
